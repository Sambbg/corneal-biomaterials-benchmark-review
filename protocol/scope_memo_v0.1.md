# Scope Memo v0.1

## Project Title

From Optical Clarity to Clinical Readiness: A Layer-Specific Benchmarking Review of Biomaterials for Corneal Tissue Engineering

## Target Journal

Primary target: Acta Biomaterialia

## Review Type

Critical narrative review with systematic search transparency.

## Core Problem

Corneal tissue engineering is a mature enough field that a broad "recent advances" review is unlikely to be publishable in a high-level journal. The field already contains many reviews describing biomaterials, scaffolds, hydrogels, decellularized matrices, cell sources, and bioprinting methods.

The publishable gap is not simply identifying what materials exist. The stronger gap is that many studies describe biomaterials as "promising" without benchmarking them consistently against the optical, mechanical, biological, and translational requirements of the cornea.

## Central Research Question

How well do currently reported biomaterials and biofabrication approaches reproduce the optical, mechanical, biological, and translational requirements of layer-specific corneal repair or replacement?

## Central Thesis

Corneal biomaterials should be evaluated through a layer-specific benchmarking framework. A material or construct is not clinically credible simply because it is cytocompatible or novel. It must satisfy the specific requirements of the target corneal layer, including transparency, biomechanics, cellular phenotype support, degradation behaviour, surgical handling, safety, manufacturability, and regulatory readiness.

## Why Acta Biomaterialia Fits

Acta Biomaterialia is a strong target because the proposed review is biomaterials-led rather than purely ophthalmology-led. The review will emphasize structure-function relationships, biomaterial performance, cell-material interactions, fabrication methods, and translational constraints.

## What This Review Is Not

This review is not:

- A general overview of corneal anatomy
- A simple list of recent studies
- A broad "recent advances in corneal tissue engineering" review
- A purely clinical ophthalmology review
- A formal systematic review with meta-analysis

## What This Review Is

This review is:

- A critical narrative review
- Search-transparent and protocol-driven
- Layer-specific
- Biomaterials-focused
- Benchmark-based
- Translationally oriented

## Main Review Sections

1. Introduction: why corneal tissue engineering needs benchmarking
2. Review methodology: databases, search strings, screening, inclusion/exclusion
3. Native corneal design requirements
4. Layer-specific evidence synthesis:
   - Epithelium / limbal epithelial niche
   - Stroma
   - Endothelium
   - Full-thickness and multi-layer constructs
5. Cross-cutting biomaterial and fabrication comparison
6. Translational bottlenecks:
   - surgical handling
   - manufacturing
   - sterilization
   - storage
   - regulatory readiness
   - clinical endpoints
7. Future benchmarking framework
8. Conclusion

## Benchmark Domains

Each directly compared primary study should be assessed against the following domains:

- Optical transparency
- Refractive or curvature relevance where reported
- Mechanical properties
- Swelling and degradation
- Cellular viability
- Phenotype maintenance
- Tissue integration
- Immune or inflammatory response
- Surgical usability
- Manufacturing scalability
- Clinical or regulatory stage

## Preliminary Inclusion Logic

Include studies that provide enough engineering, biomaterial, biological, or translational detail to support comparison.

Prioritize:

- Primary studies
- Preclinical studies with quantitative corneal outcomes
- First-in-human or clinical trial reports
- Studies from 2018 onward
- 2025-2026 studies for assignment alignment
- Studies with extractable optical, mechanical, biological, or translational performance data

## Preliminary Exclusion Logic

Exclude or de-emphasize:

- Pure drug delivery studies unless the system is a regenerative scaffold or implant
- General ophthalmology studies without engineering relevance
- Review articles as primary evidence
- Conference abstracts without sufficient methods
- Papers that call a material promising but provide no meaningful benchmarking data

## Expected Contribution

The review will contribute a structured benchmarking framework that helps readers compare corneal biomaterials by clinical and layer-specific requirements rather than by material novelty alone.

## Risk

The main risk is scope drift. If the manuscript becomes a broad survey of corneal tissue engineering, it will lose novelty and become weak for a high-level journal.

## Non-Negotiable Rule

Every section must support the central thesis: corneal biomaterials need layer-specific benchmarking against optical, mechanical, biological, and translational requirements.
