## Abstract

**Objective.** This report reviews the recent development of corneal tissue engineering, covering the anatomy and physiology of the cornea, the biological and mechanical properties each corneal layer must meet, and the biomaterials, cell sources, and scaffold fabrication methods reported in the 2025–2026 literature. **Methods.** Cornea-focused primary literature was identified via a systematic PubMed search (four layer-specific search blocks, 1,082 unique records after deduplication, screened for relevance to corneal tissue engineering); this report concentrates specifically on the subset of 24 primary studies published in 2025–2026 across the endothelium, stroma, and epithelium/limbus, supplemented by four studies selected for in-depth case analysis. **Findings.** Decellularized/natural-extracellular-matrix materials remain the dominant biomaterial class in the most recent literature (9/24 studies), followed by natural polymers and hybrid/composite hydrogels (5/24 each); decellularization itself is the single most common fabrication route (6/24), with photocurable hydrogel crosslinking, 3D bioprinting/digital light processing, and solvent casting also well represented. Two preclinical 2025–2026 case studies — a femtosecond-laser-cut lens-capsule endothelial carrier and a digital-light-processing-bioprinted stromal hydrogel that closely matched native compressive modulus — illustrate the field's genuine progress toward native-tissue-matching performance. Two further case studies, given deeper critical treatment, examine the only published human clinical trials found in this review: a phase I–II nanostructured fibrin-agarose implant that proved safe but only "partially" effective in 5 eyes, and a randomized controlled trial in which autologous oral mucosal epithelial cell sheets nearly doubled the normal-epithelialization rate against amniotic membrane alone — together showing that genuine clinical translation in this field, where it exists at all, is currently confined almost entirely to the epithelium/limbus, and that autologous cell-sourcing strategies currently outperform allogeneic ones in the clinic. **Conclusion.** Corneal tissue engineering in 2025–2026 is converging on biomimetic, decellularization-heavy strategies with increasingly precise fabrication control, but mechanical benchmarking against native tissue remains inconsistent, and unresolved ethical questions around donor-tissue sourcing, animal-derived materials, and equitable access to advanced therapies deserve more explicit attention as the field moves toward clinical translation.

**Keywords:** corneal tissue engineering; cornea anatomy; biomaterials; scaffold fabrication; limbal stem cells; corneal endothelium; ethics

## 1. Introduction

The cornea is the eye's transparent, avascular front window, responsible for roughly two-thirds of the eye's total refractive power. Because it has no blood supply and depends on a delicate, layer-specific architecture to stay transparent, damage to any one of its layers — whether from infection, trauma, dystrophy, or surgical complication — can cause permanent vision loss unless the damaged layer is replaced. Donor corneal transplantation is the current gold standard, but global demand vastly outstrips supply: an often-cited global survey estimated 12.7 million people worldwide were awaiting corneal transplantation against a supply of roughly one donor cornea for every 70 patients in need (Gain et al., 2016). This shortage, worsened by donor-tissue quality variability and rejection risk in high-risk grafts, is the central motivation behind corneal tissue engineering: the attempt to build epithelial, stromal, or endothelial substitutes from biomaterials and cultured cells rather than relying solely on a matched human donor.

This report reviews the recent development of corneal tissue engineering in four parts. Section 2 covers the anatomy and physiology of the cornea, layer by layer. Section 3 covers the biological and mechanical properties each layer's engineered replacement must reproduce. Section 4 reviews, specifically from 2025–2026 journal articles, the biomaterials and cell sources currently being used. Section 5 reviews scaffold fabrication methods reported in the same recent window, with a critical comparison of their relative strengths and limitations. Section 6 presents four detailed case studies: two preclinical 2025–2026 studies (one endothelium-, one stroma-focused) illustrating the biomaterial and fabrication trends discussed in Sections 4–5, plus two in-depth case studies of published human clinical trials (2023–2024) given deeper treatment than the preclinical pair, since they are the only genuine clinical-translation evidence available anywhere in the literature reviewed for this report — none of the 2025–2026 studies reviewed here had yet reached clinical-trial stage. Section 7 offers a critical discussion and personal assessment of where the field stands. Section 8 discusses ethical issues specific to corneal tissue engineering, and Section 9 concludes with a view toward future trends.

## 2. Anatomy and Physiology of the Cornea

The human cornea is a five-layered structure, roughly 500–600 µm thick at the center, organized from anterior (outer) to posterior (inner) as: the corneal epithelium, Bowman's layer, the stroma, Descemet's membrane, and the corneal endothelium (Figure 1).

The **corneal epithelium** is a non-keratinized, stratified squamous epithelium roughly 40–50 µm thick, made up of about five to six cell layers that are continuously shed from the surface and regenerated from a basal stem-cell population. That stem-cell population does not sit within the epithelium itself but in a specialized ring-shaped niche at the corneal periphery called the **limbus**, where limbal epithelial stem cells (LESCs) reside alongside supporting niche cells (limbal mesenchymal stromal cells and melanocytes). The limbus is functionally critical: LESC depletion, from chemical burns, chronic inflammation, or contact-lens-related disease, causes limbal stem cell deficiency (LSCD), in which the corneal surface is progressively overgrown by conjunctival tissue, causing scarring, neovascularization, and blindness. This is why "epithelium/limbus" is treated as a single engineering target throughout this report rather than two separate ones.

**Bowman's layer**, roughly 8–15 µm thick, is an acellular, densely woven collagen layer immediately beneath the epithelium that provides mechanical protection to the underlying stroma. It is present in primates but thin or absent in many other mammals, which matters when interpreting animal-model studies.

The **stroma** makes up about 90% of total corneal thickness (roughly 470–500 µm) and is the layer principally responsible for the cornea's mechanical strength and optical transparency. It consists of several hundred parallel lamellae of highly organized type I collagen fibrils, interspersed with sparse, flattened keratocytes. Transparency depends on the fibrils being spaced with a regularity finer than the wavelength of visible light (under roughly 200 nm spacing), so that scattered light from neighboring fibrils destructively interferes; any disruption to this lattice — scarring, edema, or disorganized new collagen deposition — causes visible haze.

**Descemet's membrane**, 10–12 µm thick, is the basement membrane secreted by the corneal endothelium and thickens gradually with age. It sits directly above the **corneal endothelium**, a single, non-regenerating monolayer of hexagonal, mitochondria-rich cells roughly 4–6 µm thick. The endothelium's job is not passive: it actively pumps fluid out of the stroma via Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase ion pumps, counteracting the stroma's natural tendency to imbibe water and swell. Because human corneal endothelial cells essentially do not divide in vivo, cell density can only fall over a person's lifetime — from roughly 4,000–5,000 cells/mm² at birth to about 2,000–3,000 cells/mm² in a healthy adult — and once density drops below approximately 500–700 cells/mm², the remaining cells can no longer compensate by stretching, and the cornea decompensates into chronic edema and clouding (corneal decompensation is, together with LSCD, one of the two clinical problems corneal tissue engineering is most often trying to solve).

## 3. Biological and Mechanical Properties of Corneal Tissue

Any engineered corneal-layer substitute is implicitly being benchmarked, whether the original authors state it explicitly or not, against the native physiological values summarized in Figure 1. Two points are worth making explicit because they are easy to get wrong when reading across studies.

First, **corneal mechanical testing is reported at two genuinely different scales that are not interchangeable**. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) nano-indentation gives a local elastic modulus, typically in the kilopascal (kPa) range, that reflects the cornea's surface-level micromechanics: reported layer-by-layer AFM values are epithelium 0.57 ± 0.29 kPa, anterior stroma 1.1 ± 0.6 kPa, posterior stroma 0.38 ± 0.22 kPa, Descemet's membrane 11.7 ± 7.4 kPa, and endothelium 4.1 ± 1.7 kPa (Thomasy et al., 2014). Bulk tensile testing of whole excised stromal tissue, by contrast, gives a much higher megapascal (MPa)-scale value that reflects the tissue's macroscopic load-bearing behavior: native human corneal stroma has been reported with an ultimate tensile strength of 10.6 ± 0.7 MPa and a tensile modulus generally cited in the 3–13 MPa range (Formisano et al., 2021). A paper reporting a "115 kPa" modulus for a stromal-mimicking hydrogel and a paper reporting a "13 MPa" modulus are not disagreeing with each other — they are measuring different things — and any comparison across studies needs to keep this distinction explicit, which the corneal-biomaterials literature does not always do consistently.

Second, **optical transparency is a whole-tissue property, not a single-layer average**. Spectral transmission studies of the intact human cornea show near-unity transmittance (>90%) across the visible and near-infrared range, with the stroma specifically identified as the single layer with the lowest transmittance of the five, and Bowman's layer as the most UV-absorptive (Peris-Martínez et al., 2021). A scaffold that reports high visible-light transmittance in isolation is not automatically demonstrating it will integrate transparently into a multi-layer native cornea.

Biologically, the corneal layers differ sharply in regenerative capacity, which has direct engineering implications. The epithelium regenerates readily from the limbal stem-cell niche, which is why limbal stem-cell deficiency is one of the more clinically tractable targets for cell-based therapy: epithelium/limbus scaffolds feature prominently among the 2025–2026 studies reviewed in Section 4 (Table 1), and the epithelium/limbus is, in this report's reviewed literature, the only corneal layer with published human clinical-trial evidence at all (Section 6, Case Studies 3–4). The endothelium, by contrast, essentially cannot regenerate in vivo, which is precisely why endothelial tissue engineering research is so heavily focused on ex vivo cell expansion and carrier-based delivery — the cells have to be grown and transplanted, because the patient's own endothelium cannot replace itself. The stroma sits in between: keratocytes can proliferate and remodel collagen to a limited degree, but severe stromal scarring or thinning generally requires an actual replacement scaffold rather than relying on native repair.

## 4. Recent Developments (2025–2026): Biomaterials and Cells

To ground this section specifically in current literature rather than older, more general reviews, the following draws on 24 primary corneal tissue-engineering studies published in 2025 or 2026, identified from a systematic PubMed-based screening process and classified by biomaterial class and cell source (Figure 2A; Table 1 lists the complete set of 24 studies).

**Decellularized and natural extracellular matrix (ECM) materials dominate the most recent literature (9/24 studies, Figure 2A).** This reflects a continued preference for biomimicry over synthetic-materials-first design: rather than building a scaffold from first principles, most 2025–2026 groups start from a natural tissue — porcine cornea, human amniotic membrane, or even non-corneal donor tissue — and decellularize it to leave a native-composition, cell-free matrix. Examples span an unusually wide range of source tissues. Kim et al. (2025) compared two decellularization protocols (0.3% sodium dodecyl sulfate versus 1.5 M sodium chloride) on porcine corneal ECM for endothelial regeneration, finding the SDS-based protocol gave better DNA clearance and glycosaminoglycan retention at some cost to collagen content. Zhao et al. (2025) took a hybrid approach, casting a synthetic hydrogel (polyethylene glycol diacrylate) inside a decellularized porcine corneal template, then digesting away the template to leave a hydrogel skeleton that inherited the native tissue's hierarchical microstructure. Perhaps most strikingly, Yin et al. (2025) turned to a non-ocular, non-mammalian source entirely — decellularized fish swim bladder — reporting light transmittance of 93.1 ± 0.8%, slightly exceeding native human cornea, on the logic that swim-bladder collagen's natural elastin content gives useful elasticity that purely collagen-based scaffolds lack.

**Natural polymers and hybrid/composite hydrogels are the next most common classes (5/24 each).** Gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA) appears repeatedly, usually combined with a second natural polymer through photocurable or Schiff-base chemistry to balance mechanical strength against biocompatibility: Li et al. (2025) combined GelMA with oxidized chondroitin sulfate, and Deng et al. (2025) combined GelMA with oxidized hyaluronic acid and carboxymethyl chitosan, both targeting injectable, sutureless repair of large corneal stromal/epithelial defects. Wei et al. (2025) used a related Schiff-base chemistry (oxidized guar gum crosslinked with carboxymethyl chitosan) specifically to create a self-healing adhesive hydrogel loaded with mesenchymal stem cell-derived exosomes, shifting the therapeutic payload from cells themselves to cell-derived signaling factors. On the synthetic and composite side, Peng et al. (2025) reported a graphene oxide/niobium carbide MXene nanocomposite intended as a full artificial-cornea material, evaluated for mechanical strength, corrosion resistance, and cytocompatibility — a rare example in this recent window of a fully synthetic, non-biologically-derived approach.

**Cell sourcing in the 2025–2026 literature spans donor-derived primary cells, xenogeneic (animal) cells, and induced pluripotent stem cell (iPSC)-derived cells**, with a discernible shift toward iPSC-derived and exosome/secretome-based strategies relative to older literature. Chi et al. (2025) differentiated iPSCs into human corneal endothelial cells via a two-step neural-crest-cell induction protocol and combined them with a thermosensitive hydrogel, reporting statistically significant transparency improvement in a rabbit endothelial-dysfunction model. Mahmood et al. (2026) supported iPSC-derived limbal stem cells on an electrospun scaffold (Section 5). Wei et al. (2025), as noted, moved away from delivering live cells altogether and instead delivered mesenchymal stem cell exosomes — a strategy that sidesteps some of the immunogenicity and cell-viability concerns of live-cell delivery, at the cost of a less well understood mechanism of action. Human donor tissue remains the single most common cell/tissue source across the subset, largely via decellularized-scaffold approaches, while rabbit and porcine cells remain the default for functional in vivo validation, a pattern discussed further in Section 8.

## 5. Scaffold Fabrication Methods

Fabrication method, classified across the same 24-study 2025–2026 subset, is summarized in Figure 2B. Six distinct approaches are well represented, each with a different profile of precision, scalability, and regulatory complexity.

**Decellularization** (6/24 studies, the single most common method) chemically or enzymatically strips cells and DNA from a donor tissue while attempting to preserve its native ECM composition and microarchitecture. Its central advantage is that it inherits genuinely native composition and hierarchical structure "for free" — something no synthetic fabrication method can fully replicate — and both Kim et al. (2025) and Zhao et al. (2025) illustrate this well. Its central limitation is batch-to-batch variability (decellularization efficiency depends on the specific donor tissue, and residual DNA or detergent can trigger immune reactions) and a comparatively complex regulatory pathway relative to a defined synthetic material, since a decellularized tissue product is typically classified as a more heavily regulated biologic (Section 8).

**Photocurable and chemical crosslinking hydrogel formation** (4/24 studies) — mixing a natural-polymer precursor with a photoinitiator or a chemical crosslinker (commonly Schiff-base chemistry between aldehyde and amine groups) and curing it in situ — was the second most common approach in this recent window, used by Li et al. (2025), Deng et al. (2025), and Wei et al. (2025), among others. Its main appeal is clinical practicality: an injectable, in-situ-curing hydrogel can, in principle, be applied directly to an irregular corneal defect without suturing, which matters for sutureless-repair applications. Its main limitation is that achieving both adequate mechanical strength and high optical transparency simultaneously through in-situ crosslinking is harder than achieving either alone, and several of these papers frame their specific polymer-blend choice explicitly as an attempt to solve that trade-off.

**3D bioprinting, including digital light processing (DLP)/vat photopolymerization** (3/24 studies) offers the highest geometric precision of any method reviewed here — the ability to reproduce the cornea's specific curvature and thickness profile directly, rather than approximating it — illustrated well by Chand et al. (2025), discussed in depth as Case Study 2 (Section 6). Wang et al. (2025) and Chen et al. (2026) extended bioprinting toward more biologically complex constructs, auto-bioprinting cultured limbal epithelial cell spheroids and lithography-patterned limbal organoids respectively, rather than printing acellular scaffold geometry alone. The trade-off is throughput and cost: bioprinting equipment and per-construct fabrication time are less scalable than casting or decellularization at present, which matters for eventual manufacturing at clinical scale.

**Solvent casting/molding** (3/24 studies) — pouring a liquid precursor into a mold, often a decellularized-tissue mold as in Zhao et al. (2025) — remains a comparatively simple, low-cost, low-equipment-barrier method well suited to laboratories without bioprinting infrastructure, though it offers far less fine geometric control than 3D bioprinting.

**Femtosecond laser micromachining** (1/24 studies) — used by Aouimeur et al. (2025), discussed as Case Study 1 — cuts existing biological tissue (in this case, human lens capsule) to precise dimensions using an ultrashort-pulse laser rather than building a scaffold from raw material. This is a fundamentally different design philosophy from the other methods reviewed: instead of engineering a new material's properties, it selects an already-suitable native tissue and shapes it with extreme geometric precision. **Electrospinning** (1/24 studies) — used by Mahmood et al. (2026) — produces nanofibrous scaffolds that mimic the fibrous topography of native ECM at the sub-micron scale, useful for guiding cell alignment, but frequently requires a secondary surface-functionalization step (as in that study) to overcome the inherent hydrophobicity of common electrospinning polymers like PLGA.

Taken together, no single fabrication method in this recent literature dominates on every axis simultaneously: decellularization wins on native-composition fidelity but loses on batch consistency and regulatory simplicity; bioprinting wins on geometric precision but loses on throughput; casting and photocurable hydrogels win on clinical practicality and cost but require careful chemistry to balance transparency against mechanical strength; and femtosecond laser cutting and electrospinning each solve a narrower, more specific problem (precise reshaping of existing tissue; fibrous topography, respectively) rather than functioning as general-purpose fabrication routes.

## 6. Case Studies

Case Studies 1 and 2 below are drawn from the 2025–2026 preclinical literature reviewed in Sections 4–5, chosen specifically to give one-layer-each coverage of the endothelium and stroma (the two layers with no published clinical-trial evidence at all, per Case Studies 3–4 below). Case Studies 3 and 4 are given deeper treatment than a typical preclinical proof-of-concept, because they are the only two published human clinical trials in the literature surveyed for this report — both predate the 2025–2026 window, and both, notably, are restricted to the epithelium/limbus, the only corneal layer with any published clinical-trial evidence found in this review.

### Case Study 1: Femtosecond-laser-cut lens capsule for endothelial keratoplasty (Aouimeur et al., 2025)

Aouimeur et al. (2025), from a French laboratory (Jean Monnet University, Saint-Étienne) with an established tissue-engineered endothelial keratoplasty (TEEK) research program, used a femtosecond laser to cut discs from the human anterior lens capsule — a tissue not normally associated with corneal repair — as a carrier scaffold for cultured corneal endothelial cells. Twelve TEEK constructs were prepared at varying seeding densities, and the study demonstrated that final endothelial cell density on the carrier could be deliberately controlled by varying the initial seeding density, reaching a median viability of 98% (range 83–99%) at 28 days of culture in the highest-density group.

Source: Aouimeur et al. (2025), Tissue Engineering Part A. Link: https://doi.org/10.1177/19373341251381346]

**Critical analysis.** This study is a good illustration of an underappreciated design strategy in corneal tissue engineering: rather than engineering a new material's optical and mechanical properties from scratch, it exploits an already near-ideal native tissue (the lens capsule is naturally thin, transparent, curved, and biocompatible) and applies a highly precise fabrication step (femtosecond laser cutting) to shape it. This sidesteps the optical/mechanical trade-offs that dominate synthetic and hydrogel-based approaches (Sections 4–5) almost entirely. Its main limitation is scalability and supply: unlike a hydrogel or a decellularized porcine scaffold, the human lens capsule is itself a scarce, donor-dependent material, so this approach does not solve the underlying donor-tissue shortage problem that motivates corneal tissue engineering in the first place — it relocates the shortage from donor corneas to donor lens capsules. The study also builds directly on this group's own prior published work (a rabbit surgical model and an initial carrier-characterization study), which is worth noting as an example of the kind of sustained, multi-paper translational program that is still relatively rare in this field.

### Case Study 2: DLP-bioprinted GelMA/OxiCMC stromal equivalent (Chand et al., 2025)

Chand et al. (2025) used digital light processing (DLP) — a vat-photopolymerization 3D bioprinting technique — to fabricate a biomimetic corneal stroma equivalent from a gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA)/oxidized carboxymethylcellulose (OxiCMC) interpenetrating-network hydrogel, dual-crosslinked via Schiff-base reaction and photocrosslinking, using tartrazine as a photoabsorber to control print resolution. The printed construct achieved a compressive modulus of 106.3 kPa, closely matching a reported native corneal stroma benchmark of 115.3 kPa in the same study, alongside native-mimicking curvature, thickness geometry, and optical transparency.

Source: Chand et al. (2025), Biofabrication. Link: https://doi.org/10.1088/1758-5090/adab27]

**Critical analysis.** This is one of the more quantitatively convincing studies in the recent literature specifically because it reports a bioprinted construct's modulus directly alongside a stated native benchmark rather than only in isolation — the kind of explicit, apples-to-apples comparison Section 3 argues the field should do more consistently. That said, the modulus reported (106.3 kPa, compressive) is on the same order of magnitude as the AFM-scale native values compiled in Figure 1 (posterior stroma 0.38 kPa, anterior stroma 1.1 kPa) but nowhere close to the bulk tensile modulus benchmark (3–13 MPa; Formisano et al., 2021) — which measurement the study's own "native corneal stroma" comparator actually corresponds to is not fully disambiguated from the information available in the abstract, and this is exactly the kind of AFM-versus-bulk-tensile ambiguity flagged in Section 3. DLP bioprinting's real strength here is geometric fidelity (curvature and thickness matched to native anatomy directly, rather than approximated by casting), which is a genuine advantage over simpler fabrication routes for a layer where precise geometry matters as much as bulk material properties.

### Case Study 3: Nanostructured fibrin-agarose allogeneic implant, phase I–II clinical trial (González-Gallardo et al., 2023)

González-Gallardo et al. (2023) surgically applied a genuinely tissue-engineered construct — a nanostructured (compacted) fibrin-agarose hydrogel scaffold seeded with allogeneic (donor-derived) human corneal epithelial and stromal cells, marketed under the name NANOULCOR — to 5 eyes with severe trophic corneal ulcers combining stromal degradation/fibrosis and limbal stem cell deficiency, in an interim phase I–II clinical trial. The implant fully covered the corneal surface and reduced ocular surface inflammation, and the scaffold itself was fully bioabsorbed within 3–12 weeks post-surgery, leaving the patient's own regenerating tissue in its place rather than a permanent foreign material. Over a minimum 2-year follow-up, there was no graft rejection, infection, neovascularization, detachment, or ulcer relapse in any of the 5 eyes, with only 4 non-severe adverse reactions recorded across the whole cohort, and the treated eyes showed significant improvement on standardized eye-complication grading scales. The authors themselves, however, describe the achieved efficacy as only "partial."

[FIGURE PLACEHOLDER: Figure 5 — insert before/after slit-lamp clinical photographs of a treated eye and/or the paper's schematic of the nanostructured fibrin-agarose (NANOULCOR) scaffold structure. Source: González-Gallardo et al. (2023), Biomedicine & Pharmacotherapy. Link: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopha.2023.114612]

**Critical analysis.** This is the most directly relevant clinical case study to this report's scope, because it is not a carrier-free cell sheet but an actual defined-biomaterial scaffold (a natural-polymer hydrogel, the same broad category discussed throughout Sections 4–5) seeded with allogeneic cells and taken all the way to human implantation — a genuine bench-to-bedside example of the kind of construct this report otherwise only discusses at the preclinical stage. It also treats a more complex indication than isolated limbal stem cell deficiency: combined stromal degradation and limbal disease, which is closer to the kind of severe, multi-layer damage that motivates this field in the first place (Section 1), rather than a single, cleanly defined defect. The safety profile is, on its own terms, genuinely strong — zero rejection or infection events, and a scaffold that fully resorbs within three months rather than persisting as permanent foreign material is a meaningful design feature, since it avoids the long-term biomaterial-related complications (chronic inflammation, mechanical mismatch) that can affect permanently implanted constructs. But "safe and feasible" is doing more work here than "effective": the authors' own "partial" efficacy language, combined with n=5 eyes, no control group, and no randomization, means this trial demonstrates that the approach does not obviously harm patients, not that it reliably restores vision-relevant corneal function — an important distinction that is easy to blur when a paper's title says "successful." It also, like Case Study 1, still depends on allogeneic donor-derived cells, so it does not escape the donor-supply problem, it only relocates part of it from whole-cornea transplantation to cell sourcing; and no quantitative optical transmittance or mechanical/biomechanical outcome data were reported, so it is not possible to compare this construct's functional performance against the native-tissue benchmarks established in Section 3.

### Case Study 4: Randomized controlled trial of autologous oral mucosal epithelial cell sheets for limbal stem cell deficiency (Gao et al., 2024)

Gao et al. (2024) ran a randomized clinical comparison in patients with limbal stem cell deficiency, assigning one group to receive cultured autologous oral mucosal epithelial cell sheet (CAOMECS) transplantation combined with amniotic membrane, and a control group to receive amniotic membrane transplantation alone. Over a 6-month follow-up (with an interim assessment at 3 months), the CAOMECS + amniotic membrane group achieved a substantially higher rate of normal corneal epithelialization than amniotic membrane alone (73.33% versus 35.48%), healed faster (3.45 versus 4.64 weeks to complete epithelialization), had better corneal transparency, showed a lower corneal neovascularization score at 6 months (1.47 versus 1.94), and had less recurrence of symblepharon (scarring adhesion between the eyelid and eyeball) — a broader and more consistently favorable outcome profile than a single headline statistic suggests.

Source: Gao et al. (2024), Archives of Medical Science. Link: https://doi.org/10.5114/aoms/115576]

**Critical analysis.** Of all four case studies in this report, this is the strongest piece of quantitative clinical evidence, and it is worth being specific about why: it is randomized, it has a comparator arm rather than a single uncontrolled group, and it reports several converging outcome measures — epithelialization rate, healing speed, transparency, neovascularization score, and symblepharon recurrence — that all point in the same direction, rather than a single favorable number that could be a statistical artifact. It is also, in my view, the most scalable clinical approach reviewed in this entire report, because the cell source is autologous (the patient's own oral mucosa) — this simultaneously avoids the donor-cornea shortage discussed in Section 1 and the allogeneic-rejection risk that Case Study 3 explicitly has to monitor for, without needing to demonstrate mere absence-of-rejection as its main safety claim the way Case Study 3 does. The limitations are still real and worth stating plainly: the retrieved trial data does not report per-group sample size or full inferential statistics (p-values, confidence intervals), so the size of these effects cannot be independently judged for statistical robustness; best-corrected visual acuity was listed as an outcome measure but its result is not reported, meaning even this comparatively strong trial does not yet establish a functional vision benefit, only anatomical and surface-healing benefits; and 6 months is a short follow-up for judging whether the epithelialization advantage persists long-term or whether the underlying limbal stem cell deficiency eventually recurs. Taken together with Case Study 3, these two trials suggest that autologous, cell-sheet-based strategies are currently closer to convincing clinical evidence than scaffold-based allogeneic implants — a pattern that lines up with, and adds real clinical weight to, the cell-sourcing argument I make in Section 7.

## 7. Critical Discussion and Personal Assessment

Having reviewed the anatomy, properties, recent biomaterials, and fabrication methods above, I think three patterns in the 2025–2026 literature are worth stating plainly rather than only implying.

First, **the field's continued preference for decellularized and natural-ECM materials (Section 4; Figure 2A) is, in my assessment, a reasonable but somewhat conservative choice that is starting to show its limits.** It works well precisely because native tissue composition is hard to beat on biocompatibility and biomimicry, which is why it remains the dominant strategy. But it structurally cannot solve the donor-tissue shortage that motivates this entire field in the first place (Section 1) — Case Study 1 and both clinical trials (Case Studies 3–4) all still depend on a supply of donor tissue or donor-derived cells somewhere upstream, and even Case Study 3, the closer of the two trials to a true "engineered scaffold," is built on allogeneic donor cells rather than a donor-independent source. I would argue the studies that matter most for the field's long-term trajectory are the minority working on fully synthetic or fully defined-cell-source approaches (like Mahmood et al.'s iPSC-seeded electrospun scaffold in Section 5, or the graphene oxide/MXene composite discussed in Section 4), even though these are currently harder to get working and less represented in the literature, precisely because they are the only category of approach that could, in principle, scale independently of donor supply.

Second, **mechanical characterization remains the field's weakest and most inconsistently reported benchmark**, and Case Study 2 illustrates this well even in an otherwise strong paper: reporting a modulus value next to a native benchmark is good practice, but without clarity on whether that benchmark is an AFM-scale or bulk-tensile-scale measurement (Section 3), the comparison is harder to trust than it first appears. I think reviewers and journals in this specific subfield should start requiring authors to state explicitly which measurement scale they are using and why, given how easy it is for a modulus number to look impressively "close to native" while actually describing a different physical property than the one being compared against.

Third, **cell sourcing is quietly becoming the more interesting axis of innovation than scaffold material itself.** The shift toward iPSC-derived cells (Chi et al., 2025; Mahmood et al., 2026) and exosome/secretome-based delivery (Wei et al., 2025) in this recent window suggests some groups are starting to treat the scaffold as a solved-enough problem and are focusing instead on what cell population, or cell-derived signal, actually drives functional recovery. If that trend continues, I would expect the next two to three years of literature to show relatively less scaffold-material novelty and relatively more attention to cell-sourcing strategy and to combination approaches (a known-good scaffold paired with a better-defined cell population), rather than an ever-expanding menu of new hydrogel chemistries. The two clinical-trial case studies in Section 6 add real-world weight to this argument rather than just preclinical speculation: the trial using autologous cells (Case Study 4, Gao et al., 2024) reports a substantially larger, more consistent, multi-outcome treatment effect than the trial using allogeneic donor-derived cells on a bioengineered scaffold (Case Study 3, González-Gallardo et al., 2023), which — while only two trials, and not a controlled comparison of each other — is at least suggestive evidence that cell-sourcing choice, not just scaffold design, is already influencing clinical outcomes today, not only in future preclinical work.

## 8. Ethical Issues in Corneal Tissue Engineering

Corneal tissue engineering raises several ethical questions that are easy to skip over in a purely technical review but that matter for how this research should ultimately be deployed.

**Donor tissue sourcing and consent.** A large share of the recent literature reviewed here, including three of this report's four case studies (Case Studies 1, 3, and 4), depends on some form of human donor material — a donor lens capsule, allogeneic donor-derived corneal cells, or an amniotic membrane carrier. This tissue is generally obtained through eye-banking and organ-donation systems that depend on informed consent (from the donor before death, or next of kin after), and the same severe global donor shortage that motivates this field (Section 1) also means any tissue-engineering approach that still consumes donor tissue, even a different donor tissue than a whole cornea (as in Case Study 1's use of lens capsules), only partially relieves the underlying supply pressure rather than eliminating it.

**Animal-derived materials and xenogeneic risk.** A substantial fraction of the biomaterials reviewed in Section 4 are of animal origin — porcine cornea, porcine Descemet membrane, fish swim bladder, silk fibroin from silkworms — reflecting the field's continued reliance on xenogeneic sourcing even where fully synthetic alternatives exist. Animal-derived materials carry a genuine, non-hypothetical risk of zoonotic disease transmission and immunogenic reaction to residual non-human proteins, which is precisely why decellularization protocols are validated so carefully for DNA and antigen clearance (Section 5). There is also a cultural and religious dimension worth naming directly given this report's own institutional context: porcine-derived biomaterials, which appear repeatedly in the literature reviewed here, may raise acceptability concerns for patients in Muslim-majority and other communities with religious restrictions on porcine-derived products, and clinical translation of porcine-sourced corneal biomaterials should not assume this concern is negligible or purely theoretical.

**Stem cell sourcing.** iPSC-derived cells (Section 4: Chi et al., 2025; Mahmood et al., 2026) sidestep the specific ethical controversy attached to embryonic stem cells, since iPSCs are reprogrammed from adult somatic cells rather than derived from embryos, and none of the 2025–2026 studies reviewed here use embryonic stem cells. iPSC use is not ethically cost-free, however: it still requires informed consent from the somatic-cell donor, and the reprogramming and differentiation process carries a documented risk of genomic instability or incomplete differentiation that has direct patient-safety implications if undifferentiated or partially reprogrammed cells are transplanted.

**Animal testing.** Nearly every in vivo study discussed in this report used a rabbit or, less often, porcine or feline animal model (Case Study 1's rabbit-model lineage is typical of the field). Animal testing at this scale raises standard 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) considerations, and ex vivo human-organ-culture models used by some recent studies — for example, Tsai et al. (2025), who transplanted a plastic-compressed collagen matrix seeded with porcine corneal endothelial cells directly onto endothelium-wounded donor human corneas maintained in organ culture, rather than into a live animal — represent a genuinely encouraging trend toward reducing reliance on live-animal models where a human ex vivo alternative is available and scientifically adequate.

**Regulatory burden and equitable access.** Advanced cell- and tissue-based therapies of the kind reviewed here are typically classified as Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs) in the EU or Human Cells, Tissues, and Cellular and Tissue-Based Products (HCT/Ps) in the US, both of which carry substantially higher manufacturing, quality-control, and approval costs than a conventional device or drug. This creates a real risk that even a scientifically successful corneal tissue-engineering product ends up accessible only in high-income health systems, which would be a genuinely troubling outcome given that the underlying donor-cornea shortage this field is trying to solve is, by most estimates, most severe in low- and middle-income countries with the least mature eye-banking infrastructure (Gain et al., 2016). In my view, this tension between technical sophistication and equitable deployment deserves at least as much attention in this field's literature as it currently receives, which — based on the 2025–2026 papers reviewed here — is very little.

## 9. Conclusion

Corneal tissue engineering in the 2025–2026 literature shows a field with genuine, measurable progress: fabrication methods have become more precise (femtosecond laser micromachining, DLP bioprinting), biomaterial strategies increasingly report quantitative comparisons against native tissue benchmarks rather than qualitative description alone, and cell-sourcing strategy is visibly diversifying beyond simple donor or xenogeneic cell expansion toward iPSC-derived and exosome-based approaches. At the same time, the field remains heavily anchored to decellularized, donor- or animal-tissue-derived materials (Section 4; Figure 2A), a strategy that is scientifically well justified for biomimicry but does not resolve the donor-tissue shortage that originally motivated this entire research area, and mechanical characterization — specifically, consistent, scale-explicit reporting of native-tissue benchmarks — remains the field's weakest point (Sections 3 and 7). Genuine human clinical evidence, meanwhile, remains scarce and layer-specific: the two clinical trials reviewed in this report (Section 6, Case Studies 3–4) both target the epithelium/limbus, and no stroma- or endothelium-focused construct discussed anywhere in this report has yet reached a published human trial, which is a real and, in my view, underdiscussed gap between preclinical promise and clinical reality for those two layers.

Looking forward, three trends seem likely to matter most over the next several years: continued growth of iPSC-derived and cell-free (exosome/secretome) delivery strategies as an alternative to donor-dependent cell sourcing; wider adoption of precision fabrication methods (bioprinting, laser micromachining) as their cost and throughput improve; and, hopefully, more explicit attention to the regulatory and equitable-access questions raised in Section 8, without which even a technically excellent engineered cornea risks remaining a high-income-country solution to a problem that is, globally, most severe where it is least affordable.
