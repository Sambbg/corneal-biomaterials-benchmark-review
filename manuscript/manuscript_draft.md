ABSTRACT

Corneal tissue engineering has produced a large and fast-growing literature of biomaterials intended to replace or repair the epithelium/limbus, stroma, or endothelium, but individual studies are usually judged by the novelty of their material or fabrication method rather than by how well they reproduce the specific optical, mechanical, and biological requirements of the corneal layer they target. This review asks a different question: how well do currently reported biomaterials and biofabrication approaches actually meet layer-specific benchmarks, and how far have they progressed toward clinical use? A PubMed search (four layer-specific blocks, 1,318 records, 1,082 unique after deduplication) plus a targeted supplementary snowball check yielded a balanced core of 248 records, of which 100 primary studies (33 endothelium, 33 epithelium/limbus, 23 stroma, 11 multiple-layer/full-thickness) received full structured extraction across optical, mechanical, biological, and translational domains. Decellularized/natural-extracellular-matrix scaffolds were the most common strategy (34/100), followed by natural polymers (19/100) and hybrid/composite materials (17/100); purely synthetic-polymer approaches were rare (3/100). Optical transparency was reported in 58/100 studies and mechanical testing in 37/100 (with a further 30/100 partially reported), but only 26/100 studies reported both together, and this rate varied sharply by layer (stroma 10/23, endothelium 8/33, multiple-layer 5/11, epithelium/limbus 3/33). Biological/cell-phenotype outcomes were reported in nearly every study (98/100), reflecting near-universal cell-culture validation but far less consistent physical characterization. Only 6/100 studies presented genuine human clinical evidence, all in epithelium/limbus or full-thickness constructs; a further 42/100 reached an animal model without clinical follow-up. Cross-referencing an independent 2025 systematic review (Anitua, Zalduendo & Alkhraisat) that used stricter animal-comparator inclusion criteria (21 studies total) confirmed that this review's broader, layer-benchmarked approach captures substantially more of the field, including quantitative mechanical data that the comparator review does not extract at all. These findings support the central argument that corneal biomaterials should be benchmarked against explicit, layer-specific optical/mechanical/biological/translational targets rather than compared only on material novelty, and identify mechanical characterization — particularly in epithelium/limbus constructs — and the near-total absence of clinical-stage mechanical data as the field's most consistent reporting gap.

**Keywords:** corneal tissue engineering; biomaterials; corneal endothelium; corneal stroma; limbal epithelial stem cells; benchmarking; translational readiness; decellularized extracellular matrix

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background and clinical need

The human cornea is a five-layered, avascular, transparent tissue whose refractive and barrier functions depend on the coordinated properties of structurally distinct layers: the corneal epithelium and Bowman's layer anteriorly, the collagen-lamellar stroma that makes up roughly 90% of corneal thickness, and Descemet's membrane with the corneal endothelium posteriorly (Peris-Martínez et al., 2021; Thomasy et al., 2014). Each layer performs a different job and is built differently to do it: the epithelium and its limbal stem-cell niche provide a renewable, tightly-jointed barrier; the stroma provides load-bearing transparency through a precisely spaced lamellar collagen lattice; and the endothelium, a non-regenerating monolayer, actively pumps fluid out of the stroma to prevent the corneal edema that follows once endothelial cell density falls below the roughly 500 cells/mm² threshold at which passive fluid leak can no longer be compensated by cell enlargement alone (native adult density is approximately 2,500-3,000 cells/mm², declining from 4,000-5,000 cells/mm² at birth). These are not interchangeable engineering targets: a construct that is mechanically adequate for a load-bearing stromal replacement may be irrelevant to an endothelial monolayer carrier, whose defining requirement is a functional ion-pump phenotype on an ultrathin, foldable substrate.

The clinical need for alternatives to donor corneal tissue is large and well documented. A global survey estimated 12.7 million people worldwide were awaiting corneal transplantation against a supply of roughly one donor cornea for every 70 patients in need, with severe shortages outside a small number of countries with mature eye-banking systems (Gain et al., 2016). This supply gap, combined with donor-tissue quality variability and rejection risk in high-risk grafts, has driven two decades of biomaterials and tissue-engineering research aimed at producing epithelial, stromal, and endothelial substitutes that do not depend on a matched human donor cornea.

### 1.2 The problem with novelty-first evaluation

The resulting literature is large, heterogeneous, and — this review argues — frequently evaluated on the wrong axis. A new hydrogel, decellularized scaffold, or bioprinted construct is commonly presented and received primarily as a novel material or fabrication method, with success implied by cell viability and qualitative "transparency was maintained" language. This framing under-weights the layer-specific, quantitative requirements a construct must actually meet to function once implanted: does it transmit enough visible light across the relevant thickness; does it withstand suturing and surgical handling; does it support the correct cell phenotype at a functionally adequate density; and has it progressed beyond in vitro proof-of-concept toward animal or human evidence. A material can be genuinely novel and still fail one or more of these layer-specific benchmarks, and a review organized around material novelty rather than layer requirements will tend to under-report exactly this kind of gap.

### 1.3 Central argument and research question

This review's central argument is that corneal tissue-engineering studies should be benchmarked against explicit, layer-specific requirements — optical clarity, biomechanics, degradation behavior, phenotype maintenance, surgical usability, safety, and translational readiness — rather than judged primarily on the novelty of the biomaterial or fabrication method used. The corresponding research question is: how well do currently reported biomaterials and biofabrication approaches reproduce the optical, mechanical, biological, and translational requirements of layer-specific corneal repair or replacement, and where does the field's evidence base concentrate or thin out along that pathway?

### 1.4 Positioning against related work

A closely related systematic review published within weeks of this review's own drafting window, Anitua, Zalduendo & Alkhraisat (2025) (*Advances in Corneal Tissue Engineering: Comparative Performance of Bioengineered Grafts in Animal Models*, PROSPERO-registered, PMID 41597366), addresses an adjacent but narrower question and is worth positioning against explicitly rather than only in passing. Anitua et al.'s inclusion criteria mandate an animal model with a healthy-eye (contralateral or lateral) comparator arm, and their protocol excludes studies without full text, with fewer than five animals, or without a healthy-eye comparison; this yields 21 included studies across all three corneal layers combined, most of them evaluated qualitatively on inflammation, fibrosis, neovascularization, transparency, thickness, and histology. Their extraction tables contain no mechanical/biomechanical property column at all. By contrast, this review's 100-record core spans in vitro, ex vivo, animal, and human clinical evidence — most of which Anitua et al.'s stricter design would exclude by construction — and treats mechanical characterization as a co-equal benchmark domain alongside optical transparency, reporting quantitative values in kPa/MPa and % transmittance directly (Table 2) alongside a dedicated cell-source comparison (Table 3) and clinical/regulatory-readiness table (Table 4) that the comparator review does not attempt. Anitua et al.'s search (PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science; single search date 19 February 2025, prior 10 years) also predates several 2025-2026 papers this review captures, including the Aouimeur et al. (2025) "Super TEEKs" endothelial-carrier study discussed in Section 4.5. Endothelium coverage illustrates the scope difference concretely: Anitua et al. include 2 endothelium studies meeting their strict criteria, against 33 in this review's core, reflecting how much in vitro endothelial characterization work the healthy-eye-comparator requirement excludes. Anitua et al. is a rigorous, narrowly-scoped animal-efficacy systematic review answering "does the graft perform like a healthy eye in vivo?"; this review answers a broader question — "does the biomaterial meet each layer's optical/mechanical/biological/translational requirements, across whatever evidence stage is available?" — and is not a replication or minor extension of that work.

### 1.5 Scope and structure

This review covers four corneal-layer targets — epithelium/limbal epithelial niche, stroma, endothelium, and full-thickness or multi-layer constructs — using primary studies published from 2018 onward, with particular attention to 2025-2026 literature. Section 2 describes the search strategy, eligibility criteria, and two-tier extraction approach, including how much of the extracted data was verified against full article text versus abstracts alone. Section 3 presents layer-by-layer and cross-cutting results across the four benchmark domains. Section 4 discusses what these patterns mean for the field, situates them against the Anitua et al. (2025) comparator review and against an illustrative same-laboratory translational case series, and states the review's limitations plainly. Section 5 concludes.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Review type

This is a critical narrative review with systematic search transparency. It is explicitly not a PRISMA-style systematic review or meta-analysis, and no claim of exhaustiveness across databases is made.

### 2.2 Search strategy and eligibility

The primary evidence source was PubMed/MEDLINE, searched using four layer-specific search blocks (epithelium/limbus, stroma, endothelium, full-thickness/multilayer), restricted to records from 1 January 2018 onward. The four blocks returned 1,318 raw records, reduced to 1,082 unique records after PMID deduplication (236 duplicates removed). Title/abstract screening excluded 354 records, carrying 728 forward for further evaluation; layer-balancing (correcting an initial imbalance between endothelium and epithelium coverage) produced a final PubMed core of 246 records. Two additional records were added via a targeted reference-list snowball check against major recent corneal tissue-engineering reviews, identifying two primary studies absent from the PubMed screening corpus (a cornea-specific adipose-stem-cell-derived stromal bioink study and a femtosecond-laser-cut lens-capsule endothelial-carrier study), bringing the total core to 248 records (Table 1). Reviews were excluded as primary evidence throughout.

A single-database approach with a bounded supplementary snowball check is a defensible design for a critical narrative review of this kind, provided the search strategy, screening rules, and limitations are stated transparently, which this review does in Section 4.7 rather than presenting the search as systematic-review-grade exhaustive.

### 2.3 Two-tier extraction strategy

Given the size of the balanced core (248 records), extraction effort was concentrated rather than spread uniformly. A priority-ordered subset of 101 records (clinical/translational studies first, then in vivo, then ex vivo, then strong in vitro studies, then supporting/background studies) received full structured extraction across every benchmark field (Tier 1); the remaining 147 records received light-touch treatment for background/contextual citation only (Tier 2) and are not part of the quantitative results below. One Tier 1 record (a narrative review on 3D bioprinting for limbal stem-cell deficiency, mistakenly captured by the search strategy) was identified during quality control as a non-primary study and reclassified as background-only, leaving 100 valid Tier 1 primary studies as this review's core evidence base (33 endothelium, 33 epithelium/limbus, 23 stroma, 11 multiple-layer/full-thickness; Table 1).

### 2.4 Benchmark domains and extraction fields

Each of the 100 Tier 1 records was extracted against a structured template covering: biomaterial category, specific materials, fabrication method, and scaffold architecture; cell type and cell source (Table 3); optical transparency reporting and quantitative detail; mechanical testing reporting and quantitative detail; biological/phenotype testing and quantitative detail; in vitro, ex vivo, animal, and clinical evidence status (Table 4); follow-up duration; main outcomes and limitations; translational readiness; and benchmarking relevance. Optical, mechanical, and biological reporting were each normalized into a four-state scale (yes / partial / unclear-or-not-reported / no-not-tested) to allow consistent cross-layer comparison (Section 3).

### 2.5 Evidence verification levels and transparency

Extraction was performed primarily from PubMed/Europe PMC abstract text; full article text (methods, results, tables, figures) was read directly for a subset of records, either because they were open access and reachable during live sessions, or because the reviewer independently sourced the PDF. Every record's evidentiary depth was tagged with an `evidence_verification_level`: **full-text verified** (article methods/results/tables read directly; 16/100 records), **abstract plus figures** (abstract and figure captions read together; 3/100 records), or **abstract only** (structured abstract text only; 81/100 records). This distinction matters for how the results below should be read: specific quantitative values attributed in the text to a full-text-verified study are transcribed directly from that study's reported results; values or patterns drawn from abstract-only records reflect what the authors chose to summarize in their abstract, which does not always include every quantitative value that appears in the paper's full tables and figures. A structural quality-control pass (independent re-fetch and diff of two records' extracted outcomes against source abstracts, plus a full sweep for missing fields, suspiciously short entries, and duplicated text across all 101 completed rows) found no evidence of fabricated or hallucinated extraction content; the corresponding limitation — most records being abstract-level rather than full-text-verified — is treated as a reporting-depth limitation, not an accuracy one, and is discussed further in Section 4.7.

Table 1 summarizes the search and screening pipeline. Table 2 presents the full comparative biomaterials extraction, grouped by corneal layer. Table 3 aggregates cell-source patterns by layer. Table 4 separates the six studies with genuine human clinical evidence from the 42 with animal-model evidence only. Figure 1 maps native corneal-layer physiological requirements (thickness, elastic modulus, transmittance, endothelial cell density) to engineering targets, using established physiological literature independent of this review's own corpus (Peris-Martínez et al., 2021; Thomasy et al., 2014; Formisano et al., 2021; Section 4.7 notes the AFM-versus-bulk-tensile scale distinction this figure makes explicit). ## 3. Results

### 3.1 Search, screening, and corpus composition

The search and screening pipeline is summarized in Table 1. Of the 248-record balanced core, 100 records received full Tier 1 extraction and form this review's quantitative evidence base: 33 endothelium, 33 epithelium/limbus, 23 stroma, and 11 multiple-layer/full-thickness studies (Table 2).

### 3.2 Optical transparency reporting

Across all 100 studies, optical transparency was reported as clearly demonstrated ("yes") in 58 studies, partially reported in 3, and unclear/not reported or explicitly not tested in the remaining 39 (22 unclear/not reported, 17 not tested or not applicable). Reporting varied by layer: stroma studies reported optical transparency most consistently (17/23 yes), reflecting that transparency is close to the stroma's defining functional requirement and is therefore hard for a stromal-replacement study to omit; endothelium studies followed (21/33 yes) — plausible given that endothelial constructs are typically thin carriers whose transparency is a visible qualitative endpoint even when not quantitatively measured; epithelium/limbus studies reported optical transparency least consistently (14/33 yes, with 14/33 explicitly not tested or not applicable), consistent with a layer where barrier and regenerative function, not transparency per se, is the primary engineering target; multiple-layer/full-thickness studies fell in between (6/11 yes).

### 3.3 Mechanical characterization

Mechanical testing was reported as clearly demonstrated in 37/100 studies, partially reported in 30/100, and unclear/not reported or not tested in the remaining 33/100 (16 unclear/not reported, 17 not tested or not applicable). This is markedly less consistent than optical reporting and again varies sharply by layer: stroma again leads (14/23 yes, a further 7/23 partial — 21/23 studies report some mechanical data), consistent with the stroma's load-bearing structural role; endothelium studies split roughly evenly between yes (12/33) and partial (12/33); epithelium/limbus studies report mechanical data least often (6/33 yes, 7/33 partial, with 15/33 explicitly not tested or not applicable) — a pattern this review returns to in Section 4.1, since several epithelium/limbus studies with the strongest clinical evidence (Section 3.7) report no bench-level mechanical data at all because they use cell sheets without a synthetic or biologic carrier to test.

Where full-text-verified studies report specific mechanical values, the numbers illustrate how close some constructs already come to native benchmarks and how far others remain. Zhang et al. (2022), reporting an ultrathin (20 µm) acellular porcine corneal stroma (APCS) endothelial carrier, measured tensile strength of 4.463 ± 0.428 MPa versus 5.357 ± 0.574 MPa for native porcine corneal stroma (no statistically significant difference), with reseeded endothelial cell density of 3,726 ± 223 cells/mm² — comfortably above the ~500 cells/mm² clinical decompensation threshold. Liu et al. (2019), engineering a corneal endothelial sheet, reported complex tension (handling strength) of 20.02 ± 1.05 g for the engineered construct versus 20.50 ± 0.66 g for fresh native porcine Descemet-membrane-endothelium complex (P > 0.05) — a direct, favorable comparison against native tissue handling properties rather than an isolated modulus value. By contrast, Sanie-Jahromi et al. (2020) reported a polycaprolactone limbal scaffold tensile strength of only 1.7 MPa with 55% maximum elongation, well below the native corneal stroma benchmarks compiled in Figure 1 (bulk tensile modulus on the order of 3-13 MPa, ultimate tensile strength 10.6 ± 0.7 MPa; Formisano et al., 2021) — illustrating that even full-text-verified mechanical data does not automatically mean native-equivalent performance.

Only 26/100 studies reported both optical transparency and mechanical testing together as clear "yes" results, and this combined-reporting rate also varies substantially by layer: stroma 10/23 (43%), endothelium 8/33 (24%), multiple-layer/full-thickness 5/11 (45%), and epithelium/limbus 3/33 (9%). Conversely, 31/100 studies report neither domain as a clear "yes."

### 3.4 Biological and phenotype outcomes

Biological or cell-phenotype testing was reported in 98/100 studies (with the remaining 2 partially reported), making it by far the most consistently reported benchmark domain across the entire corpus. This near-universal reporting reflects that cell-culture-level validation (viability, marker expression, morphology) is close to a baseline requirement for publication in this field, in a way that optical and especially mechanical bench characterization currently is not.

### 3.5 Biomaterial-class landscape (Figure 2)

Classifying the 100 studies' reported biomaterial category into six broad classes shows decellularized/natural-extracellular-matrix scaffolds (including decellularized corneal, amniotic-membrane, and other tissue-derived acellular matrices) as the single most common strategy, at 34/100 studies, followed by natural polymers (collagen, gelatin, silk, chitosan, fibrin, hyaluronic acid, alginate; 19/100), hybrid/composite materials (17/100), materials not clearly classifiable from the extracted free text (16/100), cell-sheet/scaffold-free approaches (11/100), and purely synthetic polymers (PCL, PEG, PLGA, PVA and similar, without a natural-polymer or composite component; 3/100). The rarity of synthetic-only approaches, against a clear majority-class preference for decellularized/natural-ECM and natural-polymer strategies, indicates the field has largely converged on biomimicry-first material selection rather than a synthetic-materials-first engineering approach, a pattern discussed further in Section 4.2.

### 3.6 Cell-source patterns by layer (Table 3)

Animal-derived (xenogeneic) primary cells were the single largest cell-source category in all four layers, though by a narrow margin in endothelium: endothelium (10/33, 30%, versus 9/33, 27% human primary/donor cells), epithelium/limbus (10/33, 30%, versus 7/33, 21% human primary/donor cells), stroma (11/23, 48%), and multiple-layer/full-thickness (5/11, 45%). Pluripotent stem-cell-derived sources (iPSC/ESC) appeared in a minority of studies but were notably more common in epithelium/limbus (5/33, 15%) than endothelium (3/33, 9%) or stroma (0/23), plausibly reflecting the relative maturity of iPSC-to-limbal-epithelial differentiation protocols relative to iPSC-to-keratocyte protocols at the time of this literature.

### 3.7 Clinical and regulatory readiness (Table 4, Figure 3)

Only 6/100 studies presented genuine human clinical evidence, and all six were in the epithelium/limbus (5/6) or multiple-layer/full-thickness (1/6) categories — no endothelium or stroma study in this corpus reached documented human clinical evidence. The clinical-evidence records include a prospective clinical trial of biomaterial-free cultured oral mucosal epithelial cell sheets (Kim et al., 2018c) with 6-month follow-up; a first-in-human case series using an animal-product-free cultured autologous oral mucosal epithelial protocol with follow-up extending to 34 months (Case 1) and up to 4 years (Ma et al., 2021); a randomized comparative human trial of cultured autologous oral mucosal epithelial cell sheets (Gao et al., 2024); a prospective randomized controlled trial comparing two ex vivo tissue-engineered epithelial constructs (Tandon et al., 2024); an early single-case clinical comparison of commercial amniotic-membrane products (Mao et al., 2023); and a phase I-II clinical trial of the NANOULCOR nanostructured fibrin-agarose construct for full-thickness reconstruction, with a minimum 2-year clinical follow-up (González-Gallardo et al., 2023). Notably, none of these six clinical-evidence studies report bench-level mechanical characterization data — several explicitly note that, as cell-sheet constructs without a synthetic or biologic carrier, there is no discrete material to mechanically test — reinforcing the pattern identified in Section 3.3 that the field's most clinically advanced constructs are also its least mechanically characterized.

A further 42/100 studies reached an animal model without documented human clinical evidence, spread across all four layers (11 endothelium, 11 epithelium/limbus, 14 stroma, 6 multiple-layer/full-thickness, by direct count from Table 4), leaving 52/100 studies at in vitro or ex vivo stage only (22 endothelium, 17 epithelium/limbus, 9 stroma, 4 multiple-layer/full-thickness). This distribution — a broad base of in vitro and animal-model work narrowing sharply toward a small clinical apex concentrated in one layer — is visualized in Figure 3 and discussed as a translational bottleneck in Section 4.3.

### 3.8 Illustrative translational case series: the Saint-Étienne TEEK program

One same-laboratory sequence of studies in this corpus illustrates layer-specific translational progression concretely. Ben Moussa et al. (2024) reported femtosecond-laser cutting and decellularization of the human anterior lens capsule as an endothelial-keratoplasty carrier, building on an earlier surgical-technique paper from the same group establishing the rabbit TEEK (tissue-engineered endothelial keratoplasty) model (Crouzet et al., 2022, not itself a benchmarking-table entry in this corpus since it reports no optical, mechanical, or biological characterization data — see Section 4.5). Aouimeur et al. (2025), from the same laboratory, then reported a controlled-cell-density iteration of the same lens-capsule-carrier approach ("Super TEEKs"), reaching median endothelial viability of 98% (range 83-99%) at 28 days of culture and citing both the Crouzet and Ben Moussa papers as direct methodological precedents. This three-paper lineage — surgical-model establishment, carrier-material characterization, then controlled-density refinement — is a useful illustration of how a single translational program accumulates evidence across the benchmark domains this review tracks, and is discussed further in Section 4.5.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Layer-specific synthesis against native requirements

Read against the native physiological benchmarks compiled in Figure 1, the results in Section 3 suggest each corneal layer's tissue-engineering literature has developed a different characteristic gap. **Endothelium** attracted the joint-largest number of Tier 1 studies (33, tied with epithelium/limbus) and a comparatively high rate of mechanical reporting (12/33 "yes" plus 12/33 "partial" — 24/33 reporting some mechanical data), yet only 8/33 (24%) endothelium studies report both optical and mechanical characterization together, and functional-equivalence claims are frequently made using non-standardized proxies — corneal-thickness/edema time-course (a functional pump-activity proxy, as in Tsai et al., 2025) or complex-tension handling comparisons against native tissue (Liu et al., 2019) — rather than a shared quantitative modulus or transmittance benchmark. This heterogeneity of endpoints makes cross-study comparison within the endothelium literature harder than the raw study count would suggest. **Epithelium/limbus** shows the inverse pattern: it is the layer with the most human clinical evidence (5/6 clinical records) but the least mechanical characterization (3/33 studies reporting both optical and mechanical data, the lowest combined rate of any layer), because its most clinically mature constructs are carrier-free cell sheets with no discrete scaffold material to mechanically test — a legitimate, layer-appropriate design choice, not a reporting failure, but one that means "mechanical benchmarking" as a evaluative concept applies unevenly across layers rather than uniformly. **Stroma** shows the most balanced benchmark coverage (17/23 optical yes, 21/23 some mechanical data, 10/23 both together), consistent with transparency and load-bearing mechanics both being close to the stroma's core functional definition, and this corpus's full-text-verified stromal example (Hashimoto et al., 2024) comes closest among all layers to reporting native-equivalent quantitative values directly against native tissue controls — a pattern echoed in endothelium by the stroma-derived acellular carrier reported in Zhang et al. (2022; Section 3.3), which likewise benchmarked tensile strength directly against native tissue. **Multiple-layer/full-thickness** constructs are the smallest group (11 studies) but include the corpus's only clinical trial with quantitative multi-year follow-up (González-Gallardo et al., 2023), suggesting that whole-cornea or multi-layer approaches, while less numerous, are not necessarily behind single-layer approaches in translational stage.

### 4.2 A biomimicry-first material landscape

The biomaterial-class distribution (Section 3.5; Figure 2) — decellularized/natural-ECM as the largest single class (34%), natural polymers second (19%), purely synthetic polymers rare (3%) — indicates the field has converged on a biomimicry-first strategy: starting from a natural or decellularized scaffold and modifying it, rather than building a synthetic scaffold from first principles and tuning it toward native properties. This is a defensible strategy given the cornea's structurally specific requirements (Section 1.1), but it does carry a translational cost that is largely invisible in study-level novelty framing: decellularized and other biologically-sourced materials generally carry more complex, batch-variable manufacturing and regulatory pathways than a synthetic polymer of defined composition (Figure 4), a tension returned to in Section 4.6.

### 4.3 The translational bottleneck

Figure 3's distribution — 52/100 studies at in vitro/ex vivo stage, 42/100 reaching an animal model, only 6/100 with human clinical evidence, and that clinical evidence concentrated almost entirely in one layer (epithelium/limbus) — is this review's clearest quantitative statement of the field's translational bottleneck. It is not simply that clinical evidence is rare in absolute terms; it is that the layers with the most animal-model work (stroma, endothelium) have produced comparatively little clinical evidence relative to their preclinical volume, while the layer with the most clinical evidence (epithelium/limbus) achieved it disproportionately through carrier-free cell-sheet approaches that sidestep, rather than solve, the mechanical-characterization question this review otherwise treats as a core benchmark domain. This suggests two somewhat independent translational pathways coexist in the current literature — a materials-and-mechanics-forward pathway (strongest in stroma and endothelium) and a cell-biology-forward, carrier-minimal pathway (strongest in epithelium/limbus) — rather than a single maturing pipeline that all four layers are progressing along at comparable rates.

### 4.4 Positioning against Anitua et al. (2025)

As detailed in Section 1.4, Anitua, Zalduendo & Alkhraisat (2025) answer a narrower, animal-efficacy-focused question using stricter inclusion criteria (21 studies, healthy-eye comparator required, no mechanical-property extraction) than this review's 100-record, layer-benchmarked approach. The practical implication of that scope difference is visible directly in this review's own results: had this review adopted Anitua et al.'s healthy-eye-comparator requirement, the endothelium section (33 studies, most of them in vitro or ex vivo without a healthy-eye animal comparator) would have been reduced to a handful of records, and the entire mechanical-characterization analysis in Sections 3.3 and 4.1 — arguably this review's most consistent finding — would have had no equivalent in the comparator review's extraction framework at all. The two reviews are complementary rather than competing: Anitua et al. answers "does the graft perform like a healthy eye in vivo," a narrower and stricter question, well; this review answers "does the biomaterial meet each layer's benchmarks at whatever evidence stage it has reached," a broader question that surfaces the mechanical-reporting gap Anitua et al.'s design cannot see.

### 4.5 The TEEK lineage as a translational case study

The Crouzet-Ben Moussa-Aouimeur sequence described in Section 3.8 is worth discussing briefly on its own terms because it demonstrates, within a single laboratory's output, the kind of accumulating, layer-specific evidence-building this review argues the field should be benchmarked against more broadly. Crouzet et al. (2022) established and characterized the rabbit surgical model itself (graft adherence rate, clearing rate, failure-cause analysis) without reporting any of this review's four benchmark domains, which is why it is cited here as methodological lineage rather than included as a Table 2 entry (see protocol note, Section 2.3). Ben Moussa et al. (2024) then characterized the femtosecond-laser-cut lens-capsule carrier material itself. Aouimeur et al. (2025) closed the loop with a controlled-cell-density refinement reporting quantitative viability outcomes (98% median viability at 28 days). Read together, these three papers show a translational program moving deliberately from surgical feasibility, to carrier-material characterization, to controlled biological optimization — a sequence that maps cleanly onto this review's own benchmark-domain structure (translational feasibility, material characterization, biological performance) even though no single one of the three papers reports across all domains simultaneously. This is presented as an illustrative case rather than a representative one; most records in this corpus are single, standalone studies without this kind of multi-paper lineage, which is itself informative about how unusual sustained, multi-study translational programs currently are in this literature.

### 4.6 Manufacturing and regulatory readiness

Figure 4 frames the manufacturing-to-clinic pathway using standard regulatory terminology (ATMP in the EU, HCT/P in the US) alongside this corpus's own preclinical/clinical distribution (Section 3.7). No field in this corpus's extraction fields systematically captured regulatory pathway or GMP-manufacturing status as a structured variable, so Figure 4's regulatory-stage framing is descriptive scaffolding around the corpus's genuinely-extracted translational-stage data (Table 4), not itself a corpus-derived statistic — this distinction is stated explicitly because Section 4.2's observation (decellularized/biologically-sourced materials generally facing more complex regulatory pathways than defined synthetic polymers) is a reasonable inference from the field's general regulatory literature rather than a finding this review's own data collection was designed to test directly.

### 4.7 Limitations

Several limitations should be weighed when interpreting the results above. First, the search strategy is single-database (PubMed) with a bounded supplementary snowball check rather than a multi-database systematic search; while defensible for a critical narrative review of this scope (Section 2.1), it means some primary literature indexed only in Scopus, Web of Science, or Embase, or published in venues PubMed does not index, is very unlikely to appear in this corpus. Second, and most materially, 81/100 Tier 1 records were extracted at the abstract-only evidence-verification level (Section 2.5); only 16/100 were full-text verified and 3/100 were read at the abstract-plus-figures level. Every quantitative pattern reported in Section 3 that is not explicitly attributed to a named full-text-verified study should be read as reflecting what authors chose to report in their abstracts, not an independently verified read of each paper's complete tables and figures — a real reporting-depth limitation, not a fabrication risk, since a structural quality-control pass found no evidence of hallucinated or invented extraction content (Section 2.5), but a limitation nonetheless, since abstracts do not always carry every quantitative value a paper's results section reports. Third, the four-state normalization used for optical/mechanical/biological reporting (yes/partial/unclear/no) necessarily collapses a wide range of free-text reporting styles into a coarser scale, and borderline classifications (particularly "partial" versus "unclear") involve some reviewer judgment. Fourth, this review is English-language and PubMed-indexed only, and cannot rule out a language or database-indexing bias in which materials or approaches are captured. Fifth, the clinical-evidence base identified here (6/100) is small enough that layer-level clinical comparisons (Section 3.7) should be read as descriptive of this specific corpus rather than as a generalizable claim about clinical-stage prevalence across the whole field. Sixth, Figure 1's native-layer reference values are drawn from established physiological literature independent of this review's own corpus (Section 2.6) and mix measurement scales that are not directly interchangeable — atomic force microscopy nano-indentation moduli (kPa scale, local, reported by Thomasy et al., 2014) versus bulk tensile testing moduli (MPa scale, whole-tissue, reported by Formisano et al., 2021) — a distinction this review has tried to keep explicit throughout Sections 3.3 and 4.1 rather than treating the two scales as directly comparable.

---

## 5. Conclusions

Benchmarked against layer-specific native requirements rather than judged by material novelty alone, the corneal tissue-engineering literature captured in this review (100 primary studies, 2018-2026) shows a field with strong, near-universal biological/cell-phenotype validation (98/100 studies) but far less consistent physical characterization: only 58/100 studies clearly report optical transparency, only 37/100 clearly report mechanical testing, and just 26/100 report both together — a combined rate that falls as low as 9% (3/33) in epithelium/limbus constructs, where the field's most clinically advanced work is concentrated in carrier-free cell sheets that structurally cannot be mechanically benchmarked in the same way scaffold-based constructs can. Decellularized and natural-ECM-derived materials, not synthetic polymers, dominate current material selection (34/100 versus 3/100), reflecting a biomimicry-first strategy whose manufacturing and regulatory implications (Section 4.6) are worth more explicit attention than they typically receive at the individual-study level. Translational progress remains concentrated at the in vitro/animal stage (94/100 studies), with genuine human clinical evidence still limited to 6/100 studies, all in epithelium/limbus or full-thickness constructs. These findings support treating layer-specific, multi-domain benchmarking — rather than material novelty — as the appropriate lens for evaluating individual studies and for identifying where the field's evidence base is thin: most acutely, mechanical characterization in epithelium/limbus constructs, and quantitative bench data of any kind in the small set of studies that have already reached human clinical evidence.
